/ 00 - OVERVIEW

32-part assembly. Competition-validated. Zero failures.

Owned the brake architecture, pedal box design, and sensor integration on Dearborn Electric Racing's FSAE EV. The brake pedal alone went through 7 design and analysis iterations, validated to a 2.9 factor of safety against the rule-mandated 2,000 N load. The new layout gave the driver 80mm of additional leg room and integrated cleanly with the steel space frame.

/ A1 - BRAKE PEDAL

7 iterations. 2,000 N load. 2.9 factor of safety.

Modeled in SolidWorks and validated through SolidWorks Simulation against the FSAE-mandated 2,000 N load. The final pedal endured peak braking force with a 2.9 factor of safety. Reoriented the master cylinders during the redesign to free up driver leg room. Designed with simple machining in mind so it could be fabricated in-house.

/ A2 - ACCELERATOR

Accelerator pedal - return spring, hard stop, driver-adjustable extensions.

Mirrors the brake pedal geometry on the right side with a return spring and a hard stop to bound travel. 3D-printed pedal extensions accommodated different driver heights without re-machining the main arms - useful when manufacturing time was tight.

/ B1 - MASTER CYLINDERS

Dual master cylinders - custom mounts, $2,000 total budget.

Two master cylinders, one for each brake circuit, mounted below the pedal box on weld-on steel tabs that tied into the steel space frame. Refurbished rotors and sourced calipers (AP Racing 4227 front, Magura rear) kept the entire brake system within the $2,000 budget.

/ B2 - BALANCE BAR

Adjustable front/rear brake bias - FSAE T.3.1.3 compliant.

The balance bar is a pivoting link between the brake pedal and the two master cylinders. Adjusts brake bias front-to-rear without re-plumbing the system - critical for tuning across track conditions and rule-required by FSAE T.3.1.3 (two independent hydraulic circuits).

/ C1 - APPS SENSOR MOUNT

APPS and brake pressure mounts - 3D-printed, tuned to pedal pivot axis.

Custom mounts for the Accelerator Pedal Position Sensor and brake pressure sensors, designed in coordination with the electrical and software teams. 3D-printed for fast iteration and tight geometry around the pedal pivot axis. The full pedal box assembly passed FSAE pre-competition CAD review on the first attempt.

/ 00 - THE ROLE

Differential bar - equalizing six-wheel suspension loads.

As Chassis Engineer on the Michigan Mars Rover Team, I'm designing the differential bar and chassis body components in Siemens NX. The differential bar is the equalizer in a rocker-bogie suspension - when one side climbs an obstacle, the bar transfers that motion to the opposite rocker so all six wheels stay loaded.

NXSiemens NX
6wheels equalized
URCcompetition
/ D1 - DIFFERENTIAL BAR

Reduced clearance. Tighter fits. Less lost motion.

The bar itself is a pivoting beam across the chassis spine, with each end linked to one rocker arm. The current design pass focuses on tighter machining tolerances and a redesigned joint at each end - both targeted at reducing lost motion through the equalizer.

/ D2 - CENTER PIVOT

Center pivot - bending and shear under full suspension load.

The center pivot is the only attachment point between the bar and the chassis body. It handles bending and shear under full suspension load while staying low-friction. Bearing and shaft sizing are tuned for both load capacity and smooth motion.

/ E1 - JOINTS

Rod end selection and tie-rod geometry - eliminating lost motion at the joints.

Each end of the bar connects to its rocker through a rotating joint. Redesigning these joints - picking better rod ends, tightening fits, and reworking the tie-rod geometry - was the biggest lever for reducing lost motion.

/ F1 - CHASSIS BODY

Chassis body - cross-team integration, carbon fiber and metal.

Beyond the differential bar, I'm designing chassis body components that integrate with electrical and software subsystems. Carbon fiber on the panels, metal joining where the load paths concentrate.

/ G1 - IN-HOUSE BUILD

In-house fabrication - mill, lathe, laser cutter, 3D printer.

Manufacturing parts in-house using the team's mill, lathe, laser cutter, and 3D printer. Carbon fiber layup and metal joining for the differential parts and joint rotation hardware. Designing in NX and machining the parts myself tightens the feedback loop on tolerance choices.
